Your Christmas Lights Aren’t Really Christmas Lights

In Crested Butte, your Christmas lights aren’t really Christmas lights. They go up before Thanksgiving and they don’t come down until ski season ends and the weather finally lets someone get back up to the roof to take them down, usually in May. That’s roughly six months of strands installed on your home. Whether they’re lit on any given evening is up to you, but the strands themselves are committed.

For most owners, what’s up there is whatever a holiday lighting service brought up the ladder in October. White strands along the eaves, maybe a wrapped aspen out front, the same scene as the house next door. It works, it’s pleasant enough, it fits the cozy mountain-village aesthetic that draws people here in the first place. But the look on your roof was decided by someone else.

This edition is about the alternative: permanent programmable exterior lighting. It’s installed once, integrated into your home’s architecture, and yours to control. You decide what color it is, when it’s on, and what it does. Default state is off. Designed for your specific home, ready for the many nights a year when you want the home to mark a particular occasion.
 

What It Actually Is, In Plain Terms

A low-profile aluminum track is installed along your fascia, soffits, and rooflines. Each segment houses individually-addressable LEDs that can be any color or any white temperature. The track is color-matched to your trim, so during the day it disappears. At night, it does whatever you tell it to do.

Up close in daylight you can see the fixtures sitting flush along the trim. From the driveway or the street, the track reads as part of the architecture.

What this product offers that no other exterior fixture can:

Color and color temperature on demand. Other exterior fixtures are one color forever. This track is whatever you want for the occasion.

Coordinated programming across the entire roofline. Separate fixtures can’t talk to each other. This system runs as one composition.

Animation when it’s warranted. Slow fades for a wedding. A subtle warm shimmer for the holidays. Off when you don’t want it.

Permanent install, on-demand expression. No vendor scrambles. No ladders in November. You trigger the scene from your phone, your caretaker triggers it for an arrival.

Why Most Permanent Lighting Doesn’t Survive a Winter Up Here

If you’ve tried a permanent lighting kit from Amazon or a national big-box brand, there’s a good chance you’ve already watched it fail. The freeze-thaw cycles, the UV at nine thousand feet, the dump-and-melt of spring snow, the wind that comes down off the mountain in February. These are real conditions that destroy hardware engineered somewhere milder. Most permanent lighting on the consumer market was designed by people in different climates for different climates. They’re solving a problem you don’t have, with a product that won’t last through a problem you do.

The product we install is designed and manufactured in Colorado, by a company that built it specifically for these conditions. It’s UL-listed, rated for 100,000 hours of operation, and warrantied for seven years. The cover is polymer or powder-coated metal, engineered for moisture, UV at altitude, and the freeze-thaw cycles this valley actually delivers. The control hardware sits inside the home, where the weather can’t reach it. We have not replaced a track segment due to weather, in any installation.

How It Actually Gets Used

Looking at our clients’ actual scene programming, the system spends most of its life doing one thing and is used for the others on demand:

– Most of ski season: a low warm-white wash, intentionally tuned to the home, scheduled to come on at dusk and run until a programmed off-time

– Long Crested Butte summer evenings on the deck, with a low warm-white wash that makes the exterior feel as intentional as the interior already does

– Christmas through New Year: a designed color program for the weeks when it actually means something

– A daughter’s outdoor wedding or the rehearsal dinner on the deck

– The Fourth of July, when the family is in for the parade and the fireworks afterward

– A milestone birthday or anniversary the family flies in for

– A college football Saturday, or your team’s playoff run in January

– A welcome scene the caretaker triggers for arriving guests after dark

The everyday warm-white wash is the workhorse. The occasional scenes are what make the permanent system different from a strand install.
 

A Quiet Security Benefit, Especially When You’re Away

For owners at the property only a few weeks a year, there’s a use case worth knowing about that isn’t the reason to install this system but is built in. The same programmable hardware that runs your scenes can also handle quiet security work:

– Bright white perimeter mode for specific zones, scheduled or triggered remotely by your caretaker

– Occupancy patterns during long absences, where scenes shift on a varied schedule that reads as someone being home

– An arrival scene your caretaker can trigger from their phone when family or guests are landing late

This isn’t a substitute for an actual security system. (We can do those too, separately.) It’s a quiet benefit of having a programmable perimeter already in place, with the same controls you’re already using for scenes.

Where This Sits in Your Home’s Lighting

What It Does

Tunable warm-white ambient through the whole ski season, designed for your specific home. Coordinated color and animation scenes for the many nights a year that warrant something different. Everything triggered from a single touchscreen.
 

What It Doesn’t Replace

Your landscape lighting, your architectural accent fixtures, your path lights. This is the on-demand layer that complements the always-on lighting design, not a substitute for it.
 

The Cost Question, For Context

A permanent install runs roughly the equivalent of four to six seasons of the standard ladder service. Most of our clients don’t justify it on cost. They justify it on the ability to actually mark a specific occasion, plus not having strands committed to their roof for half the year whether they’re lit or not.

When We Can Actually Install

In the Gunnison Valley, the install window for permanent exterior lighting runs from roughly mid-May through mid-October. Outside that range, weather makes the work unpredictable, impractical, and at times unsafe. Inside that range, we also work around when seasonal residents are at the property. Nobody wants a crew on their roof during the weeks they’re trying to enjoy a Crested Butte summer or fall.

If you want the system in place for the Fourth of July or a family wedding in August, the design conversation needs to happen now. If you want it in place for the holidays, our late-summer and fall calendar fills first with referrals from clients we worked with that summer, and we typically run out of installation availability before October.

The alternative is a holiday lighting service. Someone climbs your roof in October or November to clip on strands, then someone climbs back up in May once the weather allows, to take them down. Labor in this valley is what it is, which makes that round-trip more expensive than it would be almost anywhere else in the country. For the months in between, your home has strands on it whether they’re lit or not. The permanent system replaces the strands with hardware that’s invisible by day and does whatever you tell it to do at night.
 

Working with Your HOA

Most properties in the valley have an HOA or condo association with some kind of exterior lighting language in the covenants. We’ve worked with most of the boards and management companies you’d encounter in the area, and we know what each one tends to ask about and what each one usually approves.

When we design your system, we set the defaults so the standard operation falls inside whatever your covenants allow. If your board wants documentation, we’ll put together a written summary of how the system works and how we’ve configured it for your property, which you can submit ahead of an install. If you’d like to know what your HOA’s current rules are before you decide anything, ask and we can pull what we have on file.
