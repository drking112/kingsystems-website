A Different Category Than What You May Remember

If your last experience with landscape lighting was the old low-voltage kind, with bulbs that burned out seasonally, transformers that corroded, and wiring that shifted every spring thaw, the current generation is something different altogether.

The fixtures we specify are built from solid cast brass, not zinc die-cast, which corrodes. Each fixture is factory-sealed against moisture, fully closed from the day it goes in the ground. The light they produce is warm and natural, the kind that suits stone, timber, and mountain landscaping at night without flattening the warmth of the materials. If anything ever needs attention years down the line, you replace the module, not the fixture. Set on smart timers, the system runs on its own whether you're here or not.

Where Landscape Lighting Makes the Biggest Difference

Not every part of your property needs fixtures. Here are the areas where it tends to make the biggest difference.

Pathways & steps. Mountain properties often have uneven terrain, stone steps, and paths that are genuinely difficult in the dark. Good path lighting prevents falls and keeps the whole property accessible after dark, for you, your guests, and anyone checking on the home.

Patios & outdoor living areas. If you have a deck, patio, or outdoor seating area, lighting is what determines whether you actually use it in the evenings. An unlit patio gets abandoned at sunset. A well-lit one stays active for the whole night.

Stone walls & architectural details. Crested Butte's dark sky standards require fully shielded, downward-facing fixtures. It also makes for better lighting. Shielded fixtures that graze stone walls, steps, and architectural features from above create depth and texture at night without sending any light skyward.

Driveway & entry. The first thing you see when you arrive and the first thing your guests see. A lit entry says the property is maintained and cared for. It also matters when you're pulling in after a long day of travel.
 

How It Actually Gets Used

Looking at how our seasonal-resident clients actually use their landscape lighting through the year:

– The first night you arrive for the season: driving in from the airport after dark, the property is lit, the path to the door is safe, the home reads as ready.

– Long Crested Butte summer evenings on the deck or patio: the outdoor seating area stays usable past dark instead of getting abandoned at sunset.

– Family or guests arriving for an event: paths and entries are clearly visible, and the property reads as occupied and welcoming.

– The unattended months: a programmed schedule signals occupancy and supports your caretaker's site checks.

– Winter ski-season visits, when the property gets dark by 4:30pm: the system is what makes the exterior usable in the evenings.

– Service visits, deliveries, and late-night arrivals: caretakers, plow crews, and family members coming and going all benefit from a property that's lit on a schedule.

A well-designed system runs on its own. You set it once, it handles dusk-to-off automatically, and the property looks intentional whether you're there to see it or not.
 

Built for Mountain Conditions

Standard landscape lighting struggles at elevation. Temperature swings, freeze-thaw cycles, snow load, and UV intensity are harder on outdoor fixtures here than at lower elevations. The fixtures we install are built from solid cast brass, which handles thermal cycling without cracking or corroding. The brass housing itself acts as the heat sink, so there are no plastic components to degrade at altitude. Every connection in the system is sealed before it goes in the ground. No field splicing, no exposed wire ends, nothing that opens up over a winter.

We design every system to comply with Crested Butte's dark sky ordinance, which requires fully shielded fixtures that emit no light above the horizontal plane. Every fixture we specify is dark-sky rated: no upward scatter, no light trespass onto neighboring properties. It keeps the valley's night skies intact and produces cleaner lighting at ground level.

How the fixtures handle snow. The fixtures we specify carry IP67 and IP68 ratings, which means they are fully sealed and can operate while completely buried under a snowpack. Cold temperatures actually improve LED performance, so the system runs fine all winter. The real risk is mechanical: plow blades and snow blowers are harder on path lights than any weather event. We design around that from the start, routing path lights away from plow lanes and using flush-mount well lights in areas where equipment operates. Frost heave is the other factor we account for at installation, setting fixtures at the correct depth to stay stable through Colorado's deep freeze-thaw cycles. Get the design right up front and there is very little to deal with after that.

A system built for these conditions runs for decades with very little attention. For a property you're not at year-round, that's the point.

Good landscape lighting just works. It comes on every evening, looks right, and handles whatever winter throws at it. You shouldn't have to think about it.
 

Why It's Almost Always the Connections That Fail

The most common failure mode in landscape lighting isn't what you'd expect. It isn't the bulbs, and it isn't the fixtures. It's the wire connections buried in the ground.

Traditional landscape lighting systems use field-spliced connections. Someone twists a few wires together, caps them with a wire nut, slathers on silicone, and buries them in the trench. It works for a season or two. Then water gets in, copper corrodes, the connection fails, and an entire run of fixtures stops working. The fix means digging up the line, finding the failed splice, and re-doing it. In Crested Butte, with our freeze-thaw cycles, this is the failure mode that quietly kills systems.

The systems we install take a different approach. Every connection in the run is a factory-sealed plug-in connector, IP68-rated, fully waterproof and airtight, designed to handle direct burial under snowmelt, freeze-thaw, and root intrusion. No field splicing. No wire nuts. No silicone. Every connection in the system is engineered to be permanent from the moment it's made.

It's the kind of detail that doesn't show up in a brochure but determines whether a system runs reliably for fifteen years or fails in the third winter.
 

How We Install

In the Gunnison Valley, the install window for landscape work runs from roughly mid-May through mid-October. That's about five months of usable calendar each year, and we schedule the work around when you're at the property. If you're arriving in July, we can do the install in May or June. If you're at the home all summer, we'll schedule it for after you head back. Either way, you get a property that's lit and ready for your next visit.

The actual install for a typical property runs two to three days and is largely quiet. Fixtures are placed, a single trenching pass runs the low-voltage wiring, and the system is commissioned. No demolition, no major equipment, nothing left out of place. You can be on the property during the work or away.
